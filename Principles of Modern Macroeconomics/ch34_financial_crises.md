# Chapter 34 — Financial Crises and Regulation: Lessons from History

---

## 34.1 A Taxonomy

Reinhart and Rogoff (2009): five crisis types across 800 years and 66 countries. Common antecedents: rapid credit expansion ($\Delta$(credit/GDP) $>5$ p.p./year), asset price booms, current account deficits, and capital flow surges.

---

## 34.2 The Minsky Cycle

Minsky (1986): financial fragility is endogenous. The fraction of Ponzi borrowers $\psi_t$ evolves as:

$$\dot{\psi}_t = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1\hat{p}_t^A - \alpha_2 i_t, \quad \alpha_1, \alpha_2 > 0.$$

Rising asset prices attract Ponzi borrowers; the **Minsky moment** occurs when asset prices stall, Ponzi positions unravel, and a credit contraction begins.

---

## 34.3 Systemic Risk and Macroprudential Policy

Standard microprudential regulation ignores system-wide fire-sale externalities. Macroprudential tools:

- **Countercyclical capital buffers (CCBs)**: build capital in booms, release in downturns.
- **LTV and DTI caps**: limit household leverage directly.
- **SIFI surcharges**: impose additional capital on systemically important institutions.

The welfare analysis requires models with endogenous systemic risk (Bianchi, 2011) in which private agents' decisions do not internalize the social cost of correlated fire sales.
