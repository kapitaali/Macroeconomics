# Chapter 31 — Unemployment: Types, Policies, and Social Impact

---

## 31.1 Taxonomy

**Frictional:** time required to match workers and jobs. Socially efficient if the Hosios (1990) condition holds: worker bargaining power $\eta$ equals the elasticity of the matching function $\alpha$.

**Structural:** persistent skills mismatches; measured by outward shifts in the Beveridge curve at constant vacancy rates.

**Cyclical:** excess unemployment above the NAIRU during downturns; related to the output gap via Okun's Law.

---

## 31.2 Hysteresis

The hysteresis hypothesis (Blanchard and Summers, 1986):

$$\dot{u}^* = \gamma(u - u^*), \quad \gamma > 0.$$

Prolonged high unemployment atrophies human capital, erodes search intensity, and induces labor force exit. If $\gamma > 0$, a temporary negative shock permanently raises $u^*$ unless countered quickly — a strong argument for aggressive stabilization.

---

## 31.3 Optimal Unemployment Insurance

The Baily–Chetty formula balances consumption smoothing against moral hazard:

$$\frac{b^*}{w} = \frac{\gamma_c}{\gamma_c + \varepsilon_{1-e,\,b}},$$

where $\gamma_c$ is consumption-drop risk aversion and $\varepsilon_{1-e,\,b}$ is the elasticity of search intensity with respect to the replacement rate. Empirical estimates of $\varepsilon_{1-e,\,b}$ range from 0.4 to 0.9 (Chetty, 2008), implying replacement rates of 50–70% are approximately optimal for typical parameter values.
