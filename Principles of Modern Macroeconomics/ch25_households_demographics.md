# Chapter 25 — Households and Demographics: Saving, Aging, and Consumption

## 25.1 The Diamond OLG Model

At each date $t$, a young generation of mass 1 is born; the old (born at $t-1$) die. Young households work, earn $w_t$, and save $s_t$; old households consume savings and capital income. Capital market equilibrium:

$$K_{t+1} = s_t = s(w_t, r_{t+1}).$$

Combined with $w_t = w(K_t)$ and $r_{t+1} = r(K_{t+1})$, this gives an implicit map $K_{t+1} = s(w(K_t),\, r(K_{t+1}))$.

Unlike the Ramsey model, OLG can yield **dynamic inefficiency** ($r < g$): individuals save for retirement without internalizing aggregate capital accumulation. A pay-as-you-go pension system can in this case raise welfare of all generations.

## 25.2 Population Aging and Aggregate Saving

The life-cycle hypothesis implies:

$$s_{agg} = s_W\cdot(\text{working-age share}) - s_R\cdot(\text{retired share}),$$

with $s_W > 0$ and $s_R < 0$ (retirees dissave). Population aging implies declining aggregate saving and potentially current account deterioration in advanced economies.

---

## 25.3 Heterogeneous Households and High-MPC Consumers

HANK models (Kaplan, Moll, and Violante, 2018) show that the distribution of wealth critically determines the aggregate consumption response to monetary policy. Hand-to-mouth households (near-zero liquid wealth) have large MPC $\approx 1$; wealthy households have near-zero MPC. Fagereng et al. (2021), using lottery winnings for identification, estimate average MPC $\approx 0.5$ in the first year — far above the representative-agent prediction near zero.
