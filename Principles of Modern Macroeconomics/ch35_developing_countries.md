## 35.1 The Washington Consensus and Its Limits

Williamson (1990) prescribed ten reforms; the track record in Latin America and sub-Saharan Africa during the 1980s–90s was mixed. Countries that implemented the full package did not consistently achieve higher growth, partly because sequencing and institutional preconditions were neglected. Rodrik's critique: second-best problems mean that removing individual distortions can reduce welfare when complementary institutions are absent.

## 35.2 Original Sin and Debt Crises

Most EMEs cannot borrow in their own currency externally (Eichengreen and Hausmann, 2003). A 30% depreciation with a 50%-of-GDP foreign-debt ratio increases the domestic-currency debt burden by 15% of GDP — potentially triggering insolvency and requiring IMF support and debt restructuring.

## 35.3 Monetary Policy in Emerging Economies

EMEs face additional challenges: (i) exchange-rate pass-through to inflation $\approx 0.3$–$0.5$ (vs. 0.05–0.1 in advanced economies); (ii) large capital flow volatility creating sharp stabilization–exchange rate trade-offs; (iii) limited inflation credibility requiring higher sacrifice ratios. The extended Taylor rule:

$$i_t = r^n + \pi^* + \phi_\pi(\pi_t-\pi^*) + \phi_y\hat{x}_t + \phi_e(\hat{e}_t - \hat{e}^*) + \phi_{ca}(ca_t - ca^*).$$

A positive $\phi_e$ reintroduces an implicit exchange rate target, limiting monetary independence.
