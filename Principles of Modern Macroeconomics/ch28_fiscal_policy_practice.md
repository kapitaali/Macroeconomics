# Chapter 28 — Fiscal Policy in Practice: Government Spending and Taxation

## 28.1 Empirical Multiplier Estimates

Meta-analysis (Ramey, 2019): government spending multiplier 0.6–0.8 in normal times; 1.0–1.5 in recessions/ELB. Tax multiplier: $-2$ to $-3$ for permanent cuts. Transfer multiplier: 1.0–1.5 for liquidity-constrained households. Multiplier size depends critically on the monetary policy regime, degree of openness, stage of the business cycle, composition of spending, and existing debt level.

## 28.2 Automatic Stabilizers

The **structural budget balance** strips out the cyclical component:

$$\hat{s}_t = s_t - \varepsilon^s\,\hat{y}_t,$$

where $\varepsilon^s \approx 0.5$ for the U.S. Changes in $\hat{s}_t$ reflect discretionary policy; changes in $s_t - \hat{s}_t$ reflect automatic stabilization. Progressive taxes and unemployment insurance reduce the effective multiplier on output fluctuations without legislative action.

---

## 28.3 Fiscal Rules and the Fiscal Gap

The IMF fiscal gap:

$$FG = (r^{adj} - g)\,b_0 - s_0 - \text{required adjustment.}$$

Fiscal rules — constitutional debt brakes, expenditure ceilings, primary balance targets — impose binding constraints on discretion. Empirically (Bova et al., 2015), rules are associated with lower primary deficits and reduced debt accumulation, though their bite depends on enforcement mechanisms.
