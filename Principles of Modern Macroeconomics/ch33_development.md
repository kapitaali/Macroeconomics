# Chapter 33 — Economic Development: Growth, Poverty, and Structural Change

---

## 33.1 The Lewis Dual Economy

Lewis (1954): a traditional agricultural sector with surplus labor (marginal product below subsistence wage $w^s$) and a modern industrial sector. Modern-sector capital accumulation:

$$\dot{K}^M = s^M[F(K^M, L^M) - w^s L^M].$$

Growth continues until the **Lewis turning point** — when surplus agricultural labor is exhausted and wages begin to rise.

## 33.2 Poverty Traps

With hump-shaped growth dynamics $\dot{y}/y = f(y) - n$ and three fixed points, the economy settles at either $y^{low}$ or $y^{high}$ depending on initial conditions. Escaping the trap requires a **big push** (Rosenstein-Rodan, 1943; Murphy, Shleifer, and Vishny, 1989): coordinated investment across sectors to shift from the low-income basin of attraction to the high-income one.

---

## 33.3 Institutions as the Fundamental Cause of Growth

Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson (2001) use European settler mortality as an instrument for institutional quality. The IV estimate:

$$\hat{\beta}^{IV}_{institutions} \approx 1.0 \quad \text{(per unit log protection against expropriation)}.$$

Institutions — not geography per se — are the fundamental cause of large income differences across countries. This finding has shaped the policy emphasis on governance, property rights, and rule of law in development economics.
