# Chapter 18 — The Money Market: Interest Rates and Liquidity

---

## 18.1 Equilibrium

With linear money demand $L(Y,i) = kY - hi$:

$$\frac{M}{P} = kY - hi \implies i^* = \frac{kY - M/P}{h}.$$

The interest rate rises with income and falls with the real money supply.

---

## 18.2 The Liquidity Trap

At the effective lower bound $i \geq i^{ELB} \approx 0$, additional money injections are hoarded — $\partial Y/\partial M = 0$ in the standard channel. Excess money demand is satisfied by hoarding $H_t \geq 0$:

$$\frac{M}{P} = kY - h\cdot i^{ELB} + H_t.$$

Unconventional monetary policy (Chapter 29) is required to provide stimulus.

---

## 18.3 The Interbank Market

The federal funds rate equates aggregate reserve demand and supply: $R^D(i_{FF}) = R^S$. The IOER floor post-2008 binds when excess reserves are large. The **reversal rate** (Brunnermeier and Koby, 2019) is the interest rate below which further cuts reduce bank profitability enough to tighten credit — potentially reversing the sign of monetary transmission.

---

## 18.4 Monetary Transmission Channels

- **Interest rate channel**: lower $r$ raises investment and durable consumption.
- **Credit channel** (Bernanke–Gertler, 1995): lower $r$ raises collateral values and reduces the external finance premium $\rho(N_t/Q_t K_t)$.
- **Asset price channel**: lower $r$ raises equity and house prices, stimulating consumption via wealth effects.
- **Exchange rate channel**: lower $r$ depreciates the exchange rate, raising net exports.
