# Chapter 35 — Macroeconomic Policy in Developing Countries: Challenges and Strategies

---

The macroeconomic policy frameworks developed in Parts II–VI apply most cleanly to advanced economies with deep financial markets, credible central banks, stable institutions, and diversified production structures. Developing countries present a substantially more challenging environment: their central banks often lack credibility, their exchange rates are subject to sudden stops and speculative attacks, their fiscal capacity is limited by narrow tax bases, their financial markets are shallow, and their real economies are subject to large terms-of-trade shocks. This chapter examines how the standard macroeconomic frameworks must be modified to be applicable to developing country contexts, and what specific policy challenges arise.

---

## 35.1 Original Sin and the Currency-Debt Trap

One of the most fundamental constraints on developing country macroeconomic management is the inability to borrow internationally in their own currencies — the "original sin" (Eichengreen and Hausmann, 2003). When a country's external debt is denominated entirely in foreign currency (dollars, euros, yen), a real exchange rate depreciation directly raises the domestic-currency value of the debt:

$$\frac{D_t^*}{e_t} = \frac{\text{Foreign currency debt}}{\text{Domestic currency per foreign currency}}.$$

If $e_t$ rises (domestic currency depreciates) by 30%, and external debt equals 50% of GDP, the domestic-currency debt burden increases by $0.30 \times 0.50 = 15\%$ of GDP — an immediate, severe fiscal shock even if the government did nothing wrong and even if the depreciation was caused by external factors (a global risk-off episode, a U.S. rate increase, a commodity price collapse).

This currency-debt structure creates a **balance sheet vulnerability** that has no counterpart in advanced economies that borrow in their own currencies. A U.S. dollar depreciation, for instance, reduces the real burden of U.S. dollar-denominated debt (since U.S. government bonds are dollar-denominated). For countries with dollar-denominated debt, depreciation has the opposite effect — a debt amplifier rather than a debt reducer.

### Policy Responses to Original Sin

Several strategies have been deployed to mitigate original sin:

**Local currency bond market development**: encouraging domestic residents and eventually foreign investors to hold government bonds denominated in local currency, shifting the currency risk from the sovereign to foreign investors. Brazil, Mexico, India, and South Africa have successfully developed local currency government bond markets. Domestic pension funds and insurance companies create a natural domestic investor base.

**Reserve accumulation**: many EMEs, especially in Asia following the 1997–98 crisis, accumulated large foreign exchange reserves as self-insurance against sudden stops and currency crises. By 2020, China held approximately $3.4 trillion in reserves, sufficient to cover more than 15 months of imports. Reserves provide a buffer against external shocks but carry an opportunity cost (the return foregone on the reserves relative to domestic investment returns).

**Dollarization and currency boards**: some countries (Ecuador, El Salvador, Panama) have eliminated the exchange rate risk entirely by adopting the U.S. dollar as their currency. This provides exchange rate credibility but permanently eliminates monetary policy as a tool. Currency boards (Argentina 1991–2001, Estonia 1992–2010, Bulgaria) fix the exchange rate with a 100% reserve backing, constraining but not entirely eliminating monetary policy.

## 35.2 Monetary Policy in Emerging Market Economies

Monetary policy in EMEs faces challenges that do not arise in the same form in advanced economies.

### High Exchange Rate Pass-Through

As noted in Chapter 21, exchange rate pass-through (ERPT) to domestic inflation is substantially higher in EMEs than in advanced economies — approximately 0.3–0.5 in EMEs versus 0.05–0.1 in advanced economies. This means that exchange rate fluctuations have much larger implications for domestic inflation in EMEs, creating a stronger case for exchange rate stabilization as part of the monetary policy mandate.

Why is ERPT higher in EMEs? Several factors contribute: (i) a higher share of tradable goods (especially food and energy) in the consumption basket, so that import price changes translate more directly into CPI; (ii) weaker credibility of the inflation anchor, so that a depreciation generates larger expected inflation increases; (iii) dollar pricing of imports, so that EME import prices move one-for-one with the exchange rate.

The implication for the Taylor rule: EME central banks typically incorporate an exchange rate term:

$$i_t = r^n + \pi^* + \phi_\pi(\pi_t - \pi^*) + \phi_y\hat{x}_t + \phi_e\hat{e}_t,$$

where $\phi_e > 0$ captures the direct stabilization motivation for responding to exchange rate movements. This creates tension with inflation targeting: responding to exchange rate movements can conflict with the inflation target when the exchange rate depreciation is driven by supply-side fundamentals rather than demand shocks.

### Credibility Building and Sacrifice Ratios

In economies where central banks have historically accommodated inflation (or where hyperinflation episodes have severely damaged credibility), the sacrifice ratio for disinflation is much higher than in credible inflation-targeting advanced economies. The intuition from the NKPC: when $M_f \to 0$ (agents heavily discount future monetary policy announcements due to credibility failure), the behavioral NKPC becomes:

$$\hat{\pi}_t \approx \kappa\hat{x}_t,$$

with negligible forward-looking component. Disinflation requires a much larger output gap to reduce inflation than in a credible framework. Brazil's experience is illustrative: disinflation from 2000% annual inflation (1994 pre-Real Plan) to 3–10% required a dramatic policy credibility shock (the Real Plan of 1994, which introduced a new currency backed by tight fiscal and monetary commitments) rather than gradual interest rate increases.

## 35.3 Fiscal Policy Under Fiscal Dominance

**Definition (Fiscal Dominance).** **Fiscal dominance** is the regime in which the government's financing needs determine monetary policy, rather than monetary policy independently controlling inflation. Under fiscal dominance, the central bank is compelled to monetize the fiscal deficit — create money to finance it — because the government cannot access alternative financing. The Unpleasant Monetarist Arithmetic of Sargent and Wallace (1981) showed that even if the central bank today refuses to monetize, the commitment to repay debt implies future monetization is inevitable — so the price level is determined by the present value of fiscal surpluses, not solely by current monetary policy.

Many developing countries have historically operated under fiscal dominance: narrow tax bases, large informal sectors, limited domestic bond markets, and external financing constraints force governments to rely on seigniorage. The fiscal theory of the price level (Woodford, 2001) provides a general framework: the price level adjusts to ensure the real value of outstanding government debt equals the present value of future primary surpluses:

$$\frac{B_t}{P_t} = \sum_{s=0}^\infty\mathbb{E}_t\!\left[\prod_{k=0}^{s-1}R_{t+k}^{-1}\right]\! s_{t+s},$$

where $B_t/P_t$ is the real value of nominal debt outstanding and $s_{t+s}$ is the primary surplus at $t+s$. When the present value of future surpluses falls (a fiscal deterioration), $P_t$ rises — inflation is a fiscal phenomenon.

The policy implication: successful monetary stabilization in EMEs requires fiscal consolidation simultaneously with monetary tightening. This was the lesson from the European hyperinflation stabilizations of the 1920s (Sargent, 1982) and from the Brazilian Real Plan (1994): without closing the primary deficit, monetary tightening alone merely defers inflation or generates a debt crisis.

## 35.4 Capital Flow Management

Capital flows to EMEs are large, volatile, and often procyclical — amplifying booms and busts. The standard IMF view until 2012 was that capital account liberalization was unambiguously desirable, part of the Washington Consensus package. Subsequent experience and research has led to a more nuanced "Integrated Policy Framework" (IMF, 2021) recognizing that capital flow management tools (CFMs) may be appropriate in specific circumstances.

**Definition (Capital Flow Management Tool).** A **capital flow management tool (CFM)** is a policy instrument that restricts or regulates cross-border capital flows. CFMs include: reserve requirements on foreign borrowing; taxes on capital inflows (Brazil's IOF tax, 2009–12); quantitative limits on external borrowing by banks and corporates; and macroprudential measures affecting the currency and maturity structure of foreign borrowing.

### The Case for Capital Flow Management

When is CFM justified? The IMF's integrated framework identifies several circumstances:

**Volatile capital flows and financial stability**: large capital inflows can generate credit booms, asset price bubbles, and currency appreciation that erode competitiveness. CFMs can lean against these pressures when macroprudential tools and exchange rate adjustment are insufficient.

**Original sin amplification**: when capital inflows take the form of dollar-denominated debt, they amplify the balance sheet vulnerability described in Section 35.1. CFMs that favor equity over debt, or local currency over foreign currency, can reduce this vulnerability.

**Sudden stop prevention**: CFMs during booms can limit the buildup of short-term external debt that creates sudden stop vulnerability during reversals.

**The limits of CFMs**: CFMs are prone to circumvention as financial innovation creates new channels for capital flows; they can reduce financial depth and deter long-term FDI; and they may provide cover for fiscal irresponsibility (governments that cannot finance deficits through bonds resort to capital controls rather than fiscal adjustment).

## 35.5 Structural Adjustment, the IMF, and Conditionality

When EMEs face balance-of-payments crises — unable to service external debt while maintaining the exchange rate peg — they typically turn to the IMF for emergency financing. IMF programs provide short-term liquidity in exchange for **conditionality**: commitments by the borrowing government to implement economic reforms designed to restore external sustainability.

**Definition (IMF Conditionality).** **Conditionality** refers to the policy conditions attached to IMF lending, typically including: fiscal consolidation (reducing the primary deficit to restore debt sustainability), monetary tightening (raising interest rates to stabilize the exchange rate and reduce inflation), exchange rate adjustment (devaluing the currency to restore competitiveness), and structural reforms (liberalizing trade, deregulating product and labor markets, strengthening financial regulation).

Conditionality has been deeply controversial. Proponents argue it is necessary to ensure that IMF resources catalyze adjustment rather than merely postpone it, and that the conditions address the underlying causes of the crisis. Critics argue that: (i) pro-cyclical conditionality (fiscal austerity during recessions) deepens the crisis; (ii) one-size-fits-all templates ignore country-specific circumstances; (iii) conditionality undermines national sovereignty and democratic accountability.

The evidence on conditionality effects is mixed. Structural adjustment programs in Latin America and Sub-Saharan Africa in the 1980s and 1990s were often followed by slow growth and rising inequality. But the counterfactual is unclear: countries in crisis may have performed even worse without the IMF's fiscal resources and the credibility boost from the program commitment.

## 35.6 Monetary Policy Frameworks for Emerging Economies

Given the specific challenges described above, what monetary policy framework is most appropriate for EMEs?

**Inflation targeting with managed float** has become the dominant framework for middle-income EMEs: Brazil (1999), Mexico (2001), Chile (1999), Colombia (1999), South Africa (2000), South Korea (1998), Indonesia (2000). This framework allows exchange rate flexibility (helping absorb external shocks) while providing a nominal anchor (the inflation target). The central bank responds to inflation deviations from target with interest rate changes (Taylor rule) and intervenes in the foreign exchange market only to smooth excessive volatility, not to target a specific exchange rate level.

**Challenges specific to this framework in EMEs**: (i) high ERPT means exchange rate volatility directly threatens the inflation target, creating pressure for more FX intervention than a pure float; (ii) the inflation target must be set credibly — an ambitious disinflation target that is missed repeatedly destroys credibility faster than a more modest target that is consistently met; (iii) capital flow volatility creates monetary policy dilemmas where the optimal domestic interest rate conflicts with external balance considerations.

The **Integrated Policy Framework (IMF, 2021)** acknowledges that in EMEs, monetary policy, exchange rate policy, macroprudential policy, and CFMs are complementary rather than substitutable tools. The appropriate policy response to a sudden stop — for example — may require simultaneous use of all four tools: interest rate adjustment, exchange rate flexibility (not full float), macroprudential easing to support bank credit, and temporary CFMs to slow capital outflows.
