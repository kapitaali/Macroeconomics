# Chapter 19 — The Labor Market: Wage Setting and Unemployment

---

## 19.1 The WS–PS Framework

The **wage-setting (WS)** curve from Nash bargaining or efficiency wages:

$$w^{WS} = b + f(u), \quad f'(u) < 0.$$

The **price-setting (PS)** curve from markup pricing:

$$w^{PS} = \frac{F_N(K,N)}{1+\mu}.$$

Medium-run equilibrium requires WS = PS, pinning down the structural unemployment rate $u^*$ independently of inflation.

---

## 19.2 The Beveridge Curve

With matching function $m(u,v) = su^\alpha v^{1-\alpha}$ and job-destruction rate $\delta$, the steady-state Beveridge curve is:

$$u = \frac{\delta}{\delta + f(\theta)}, \quad \theta = v/u.$$

Outward shifts indicate declining matching efficiency — structural unemployment has increased. The 2021–22 U.S. labor market saw an unprecedented outward Beveridge shift, consistent with sectoral reallocation from the COVID shock.

---

## 19.3 Wage Dynamics Over the Cycle

Short run: aggregate demand shifts employment along the WS curve; the price level is sticky. Medium run: excess demand raises markups (PS shifts down) and worker bargaining power (WS shifts up). The economy returns to $u^*$ at a higher price level — the mechanism underlying the Phillips curve dynamics of Chapter 10.
