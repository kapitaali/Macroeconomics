# Chapter 32 — Open Economy Macroeconomics: Exchange Rates and Global Imbalances

---

## 32.1 The New Open Economy Macroeconomics

The NOEM (Obstfeld and Rogoff, 1995) embeds Mundell–Fleming in an optimizing DSGE model. The representative home household consumes an Armington basket:

$$c_t = \left[a^{1/\rho} c_{H,t}^{(\rho-1)/\rho} + (1-a)^{1/\rho} c_{F,t}^{(\rho-1)/\rho}\right]^{\rho/(\rho-1)},$$

with elasticity $\rho$. The real exchange rate $q_t = e_t P_t^*/P_t$ drives expenditure switching: depreciation shifts world demand toward home goods.

## 32.2 Global Imbalances and the Exorbitant Privilege

The U.S. current account deficit averaged $-4\%$ of GDP 2000–07; Asian economies ran corresponding surpluses. The U.S. earns higher returns on foreign assets than it pays on liabilities — **exorbitant privilege**:

$$r_t^{assets} - r_t^{liabilities} \equiv \Delta r_t \approx 2\text{–}3\% \text{ p.a.}$$

The U.S. provides safe assets to the world and earns a liquidity premium in return (Gourinchas and Rey, 2007).

## 32.3 Currency Crises

**First-generation** (Krugman, 1979): a speculative attack occurs when fiscal policy is inconsistent with an exchange rate peg. Reserves follow $R_t = R_0 - (\delta M/P)\cdot t/\text{credit growth}$; rational speculators attack before reserves are exhausted.

**Second-generation** (Obstfeld, 1994): the government weighs unemployment costs of defending the peg against credibility costs of devaluing. Multiple equilibria generate self-fulfilling crises unrelated to fundamentals.
