# Chapter 38 — Inequality and Macroeconomics: Distributional Effects of Policy

## 38.1 Measuring Inequality

The Gini coefficient:

$$G = 1 - \frac{2}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n\!\left(1 - \frac{\sum_{j=1}^i y_j}{\mu n}\right) = \frac{2}{n^2\mu}\sum_{i=1}^n i\,y_i - \frac{n+1}{n}.$$

Piketty and Saez (2003) document a U-shaped top income share in the U.S.: high inequality in the 1920s, compression 1930–70, rising inequality from the late 1970s. The top 1% share rose from ~8% (1970) to ~20% (2012).

## 38.2 The Kuznets Curve and Its Limits

Kuznets (1955): inequality follows an inverted-U path as development proceeds. Empirical support is fragile (Anand and Kanbur, 1993), and the U.S. experience since the 1970s contradicts the hypothesis.

## 38.3 Distributional Effects of Monetary Policy

Auclert (2019) derives a sufficient statistics formula for the aggregate consumption response:

$$\Delta C = -\mathcal{M}\cdot\Delta r + \int \mathrm{URE}(h)\cdot MPC(h)\,\mathrm{d}h\cdot(-\Delta r),$$

where $\mathcal{M}$ is the representative-agent intertemporal elasticity and $\mathrm{URE}(h)$ is household $h$'s unhedged interest rate exposure. Net debtors gain from rate cuts; net creditors lose. The aggregate effect depends on the covariance between URE and MPC across the wealth distribution.
