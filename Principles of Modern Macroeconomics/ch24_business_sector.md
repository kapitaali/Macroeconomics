# Chapter 24 — The Business Sector: Corporate Behavior and Investment Cycles

## 24.1 Corporate Finance and Macroeconomics

Modigliani–Miller (1958) capital structure irrelevance: in perfect capital markets, firm value is independent of the debt-equity mix. With taxes and bankruptcy costs:

$$V^L = V^U + \tau_c D - \text{PV(bankruptcy costs)}.$$

Macroeconomic fluctuations interact with corporate leverage through **debt overhang** (Myers, 1977): highly leveraged firms forgo positive-NPV investment because gains accrue to debt-holders. Post-credit-boom debt overhang persistently depresses investment.

## 24.2 The Financial Accelerator

Bernanke, Gertler, and Gilchrist (1999): the external finance premium $\rho(\cdot)$ decreases with net worth $N_t$. A negative demand shock reduces profits → reduces $N_t$ → raises $\rho$ → reduces investment → reduces $K_{t+1}$ and $N_{t+1}$. This self-reinforcing cycle amplifies the initial shock.

---

## 24.3 Uncertainty and Investment Cycles

Bloom (2009) documents that cross-sectional TFP dispersion — a measure of uncertainty — is countercyclical. Through the real-options channel of Chapter 12, uncertainty spikes reduce investment by approximately 1% of GDP, contributing to sharp investment collapses in recessions.
