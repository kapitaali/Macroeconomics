# Chapter 30 — Inflation and Deflation: Causes, Consequences, and Cures

---

## 30.1 Costs of Inflation

Under perfectly anticipated constant inflation, the only cost is the **shoe-leather cost** proportional to the inflation tax $\pi\cdot M/P$. Lucas (2000) estimates the welfare cost of 10% annual inflation at ~1% of GDP. Unanticipated inflation imposes distributional costs (debtors gain, creditors lose) and increases relative price dispersion, impairing the price system's allocative role (Barro, 1976).

**Hyperinflation** (Cagan's definition: monthly inflation >50%) collapses monetary exchange. The Cagan model:

$$\ln(M_t/P_t) = -\alpha\,\pi_t^e, \qquad \pi_t^e = \pi_{t-1}^e + \lambda(\pi_t - \pi_{t-1}^e).$$

Consistent across seven post-WWI hyperinflations; fiscal dominance (seigniorage as last resort) is the invariant proximate cause.

## 30.2 Costs of Deflation

Fisher's (1933) **debt-deflation spiral**: falling prices raise real debt values → forced asset sales → lower asset prices → tighter collateral constraints → further credit contraction. With household debt-to-GDP ratios of 80–100% in advanced economies, unexpected deflation of 1% raises real debt burden by 0.8–1% of GDP. The ELB compounds deflation: real rates rise automatically when the central bank cannot cut nominal rates.

---

## 30.3 Disinflation

Ball (1994) documents sacrifice ratios of 0.5–4 across 28 episodes, varying with the speed of disinflation and central bank credibility. The Volcker disinflation (1979–83): inflation fell from 14% to 3% at a sacrifice ratio of ~2 — consistent with the hybrid NKPC estimate of $\omega \approx 0.4$ and limited credibility of the early Volcker commitment.
