# Chapter 29 — Monetary Policy in Practice: Interest Rates and Quantitative Easing

---

## 29.1 Inflation Targeting

Introduced by New Zealand (1990). Core elements: a quantitative CPI target, an 18–24 month operational horizon, instrument independence, transparency through inflation reports, and accountability. Evidence (Rose, 2007): targeters achieved lower and less volatile inflation than a comparable control group, though global disinflation complicates attribution.

---

## 29.2 The Taylor Rule in Practice

Taylor (1993) parameterization: $\phi_\pi = 1.5$, $\phi_y = 0.5$, $r^n = 2\%$, $\pi^* = 2\%$. With interest-rate smoothing ($\rho_i \approx 0.85$) the policy rate exhibits strong persistence — rationalized by uncertainty about the output gap, financial stability concerns, and information signaling. The *r-star* problem: $r^n$ declined substantially post-2008, making the neutral rate difficult to estimate in real time.

---

## 29.3 Quantitative Easing

QE channels: (i) **portfolio balance** — preferred-habitat investors reallocate from safe to risky assets, compressing risk premia; (ii) **signaling** — QE signals extended low rates; (iii) **market functioning** — asset purchases reduce transaction costs. Gagnon et al. (2011): $1.75T in Fed purchases 2008–10 reduced 10-year Treasury yields by ~90 bps. Pass-through to corporate bonds was smaller due to unaddressed credit risk premia.

---

## 29.4 Average Inflation Targeting

The Fed's 2020 AIT framework commits to allowing inflation above 2% to compensate for past undershooting — equivalent to price-level targeting and the optimal commitment policy of Section 23.2. The delayed liftoff debate following the 2021–22 inflation surge is analyzed in Chapter 41.
