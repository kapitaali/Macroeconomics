# Chapter 26 — The Foreign Sector: Balance of Payments and Capital Flows

## 26.1 The Current Account

From the saving-investment identity:

$$CA_t = S_t - I_t + (T_t - G_t) = \text{national saving} - \text{national investment}.$$

The **intertemporal approach** (Sachs, 1981; Obstfeld and Rogoff, 1994) treats the CA as optimal smoothing: a transitory negative income shock induces a deficit to smooth consumption; a permanent shock changes the saving-investment balance.

---

## 26.2 Sudden Stops

Capital account openness exposes emerging economies to abrupt reversals of capital inflows (Calvo, 1998). A binding collateral constraint:

$$D_t \leq \kappa\,\theta_t K_t,$$

forces a sharp current account adjustment when country credit conditions $\theta_t$ deteriorate.

---

## 26.3 Exchange Rate Regime Choice

The **Mundell–Fleming trilemma**: a country cannot simultaneously maintain (i) fixed exchange rate, (ii) free capital mobility, (iii) independent monetary policy.

**Optimal currency area (OCA)** theory (Mundell, 1961) identifies conditions favoring a common currency: high factor mobility, high trade integration, synchronized cycles, and fiscal transfer mechanisms. Applying OCA to the eurozone reveals tensions for peripheral economies facing asymmetric shocks without exchange-rate flexibility.
