# Chapter 41 — Case Study: The COVID-19 Pandemic — Economic Shocks and Recovery

---

## 41.1 Nature of the Shock

COVID-19 was a simultaneous supply and demand shock. Lockdowns directly reduced the supply of contact-intensive services while collapsing demand for them. Standard AS–AD analysis:

$$\text{Supply shock: } \bar{Y}_t \downarrow \implies \text{LRAS shifts left.}$$
$$\text{Demand shock: } \mathcal{E}(Y,r) \downarrow \implies \text{AD shifts left.}$$

In practice, goods demand surged (home equipment, durable goods) while services supply contracted; supply-chain disruptions compounded the demand reallocation in 2021–22, producing sustained inflation.

---

## 41.2 Aggregate Magnitudes

U.S. GDP contracted 9.1% (Q4 2019–Q2 2020, annualized $-32.9\%$ in Q2 2020) — the sharpest quarterly decline in recorded history. Unemployment surged from 3.5% (Feb 2020) to 14.7% (Apr 2020). The recovery was V-shaped for output (above pre-pandemic level by Q1 2021) but K-shaped for welfare.

---

## 41.3 The Fiscal Response

U.S. fiscal measures totaled ~$5T (23% of GDP): CARES Act ($2.2T), Consolidated Appropriations Act ($900B), ARP Act ($1.9T). Key instruments:
- UI replacement rates temporarily exceeding 100% for median earners.
- Paycheck Protection Program (forgivable small business loans).
- Direct transfers of $1,200, $600, and $1,400 per eligible household.

MPC out of COVID transfers estimated at 0.5–0.6 (Parker et al. methodology), consistent with HANK model predictions and reflecting the high share of liquidity-constrained recipients.

---

## 41.4 Monetary Policy and the AIT Debate

The Fed's Average Inflation Targeting (AIT) framework (Aug 2020) committed to allowing inflation above 2% to compensate for past undershooting. Rate liftoff did not occur until March 2022, when CPI inflation was already 8%. The delayed tightening debate: was the Fed anchored by behavioral discounting of tail inflation risks (Chapter 15), or did supply-chain shocks render any policy forecast unreliable? Kiley (2022) estimates optimal liftoff would have occurred in late 2021 under the AIT framework.

---

## 41.5 Supply-Chain Disruptions and the 2021–22 Inflation Surge

Decomposing the inflation surge into demand and supply components using relative price behavior (Shapiro, 2022; Borio, Lombardi, and Zampolli, 2023): roughly 40% demand-driven, 60% supply-driven in the U.S. The supply component was transitory in some categories (semiconductors, used cars) but persistent in others (shelter, services). The Federal Reserve raised the federal funds rate from 0.25% (March 2022) to 5.5% (July 2023) at the fastest pace since 1980.
