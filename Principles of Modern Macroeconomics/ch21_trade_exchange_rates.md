# Chapter 21 — International Trade and Exchange Rates: Connecting Economies

---

## 21.1 Comparative Advantage

The Ricardian condition for specialization: country H exports good $x$ if

$$\frac{a_{Hx}}{a_{Hy}} < \frac{a_{Fx}}{a_{Fy}},$$

where $a_{ij}$ is the labor coefficient for good $i$ in country $j$. Both countries gain from specialization even if one has absolute advantage in everything.

---

## 21.2 Purchasing Power Parity

Absolute PPP: $e_t = P_t/P_t^*$. Relative PPP: $\hat{e}_t = \pi_t - \pi_t^*$.

PPP holds well at long horizons for traded goods but deviates substantially in the short run due to non-traded goods (Balassa–Samuelson), trade costs, and pricing-to-market.

---

## 21.3 Uncovered Interest Parity

$$i_t = i_t^* + \mathbb{E}_t[\hat{e}_{t+1}].$$

Covered interest parity (CIP): $i_t - i_t^* = f_t - e_t$, where $f_t$ is the forward rate. CIP holds almost exactly in normal times via riskless arbitrage; deviations emerged post-2008 as intermediary balance-sheet constraints limited arbitrage (Du, Tepper, and Verdelhan, 2018).

---

## 21.4 The Dornbusch Overshooting Model

With sticky goods prices but flexible asset prices, UIP requires:

$$i_t - i^* = -\theta(e_t - \bar{e}), \quad \theta > 0.$$

Price adjustment:

$$\dot{p}_t = \pi[y(e_t, p_t) - \bar{y}], \quad \pi > 0.$$

A monetary expansion depreciates the exchange rate immediately to $e_0 > \bar{e}_{new}$ (overshooting), then appreciates gradually along the saddle path to the new equilibrium $\bar{e}_{new}$ — a hump-shaped response replicating empirical exchange-rate dynamics.
