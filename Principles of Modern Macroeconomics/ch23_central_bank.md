## 23.1 The Taylor Rule

Taylor (1993) showed U.S. monetary policy 1987–1992 was well described by:

$$i_t = r^n + \pi^* + \phi_\pi(\pi_t - \pi^*) + \phi_y\hat{x}_t, \quad \phi_\pi > 1,\; \phi_y > 0.$$

The **Taylor principle** $\phi_\pi > 1$ ensures the real rate rises when inflation rises, stabilizing the economy. Violation leads to sunspot-driven instability.

With interest-rate smoothing ($\rho_i \approx 0.85$ estimated for the U.S.):

$$i_t = \rho_i\, i_{t-1} + (1-\rho_i)[r^n + \pi^* + \phi_\pi(\pi_t-\pi^*) + \phi_y\hat{x}_t] + \epsilon_t.$$

## 23.2 Optimal Policy in the New Keynesian Model

The central bank minimizes:

$$\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{t=0}^\infty \beta^t\bigl[\pi_t^2 + \lambda_x\hat{x}_t^2\bigr]$$

subject to the NKPC $\hat{\pi}_t = \beta\mathbb{E}_t[\hat{\pi}_{t+1}] + \kappa\hat{x}_t + u_t$. Under discretion:

$$\pi_t = -\frac{\lambda_x}{\kappa}\hat{x}_t.$$

**Divine coincidence** (no cost-push shock): stabilizing inflation and the output gap simultaneously is feasible. With cost-push shocks the central bank faces a trade-off; optimal commitment policy involves history-dependence — accepting a persistent negative output gap to reduce inflation gradually ("price-level targeting").

## 23.3 Unconventional Policy at the ELB

Three tools deployed post-2008:

**Forward guidance**: committing to low rates shifts the expected future short rate path, lowering long rates via the expectations hypothesis.

**Quantitative easing (QE)**: asset purchases reduce term premia through preferred-habitat channels (Vayanos and Vila, 2009). Gagnon et al. (2011) estimate $1.75T in Fed purchases 2008–10 reduced 10-year yields by ~90 basis points.

**Negative policy rates**: operational down to approximately $-0.5\%$ to $-1\%$ before the **reversal rate** is reached, below which lower rates tighten credit by compressing bank net interest margins.
