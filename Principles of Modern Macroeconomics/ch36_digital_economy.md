## 36.1 The Productivity Paradox

Solow's paradox (1987): rapid ICT diffusion coexisting with slow measured TFP growth. Three resolutions: (i) measurement error — quality improvements in IT goods are understated (Byrne, Fernald, and Reinsdorf, 2016); (ii) diffusion lags — GPT benefits materialize only after complementary investment in organizational capital; (iii) concentration — the 1995–2004 productivity acceleration was narrow, limited to IT-producing and IT-using service sectors.

## 36.2 General-Purpose Technologies

A GPT (Bresnahan and Trajtenberg, 1995) exhibits pervasiveness, technological dynamism, and innovation complementarities. Growth dynamics:

$$g_t = \begin{cases} g^{low} & t \in [0,T] \text{ (reorganization phase)} \\ g^{high} > g^{low} & t > T \text{ (exploitation phase).} \end{cases}$$

The AI transition may follow a similar pattern with $T$ yet to arrive (Aghion et al., 2019).

## 36.3 Automation and Labor Market Polarization

Acemoglu and Restrepo (2019): automation replaces labor in tasks previously performed by workers. The displacement effect on labor demand:

$$\frac{\partial \ln w_L}{\partial \Gamma} < 0 \quad \text{(displacement)}, \qquad \frac{\partial \ln w_L}{\partial N_{new}} > 0 \quad \text{(reinstatement)}.$$

Net effect on wages depends on relative magnitudes. Autor, Levy, and Murnane (2003) document **labor market polarization**: routine cognitive and manual tasks (middle-skill) are most susceptible, generating growth at the top (non-routine cognitive) and bottom (non-routine manual) of the skill distribution.
