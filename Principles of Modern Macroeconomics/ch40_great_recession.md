# Chapter 40 — Case Study: The Great Recession of 2008 — Causes and Policy Responses

---

## 40.1 Chronology and Magnitudes

U.S. real GDP contracted 4.3% peak-to-trough (Q4 2007–Q2 2009); unemployment rose from 4.4% (May 2007) to 10.0% (October 2009). Housing prices (Case–Shiller national) fell 33% from peak (Q2 2006) to trough (Q1 2012). The S&P 500 fell 57% from October 2007 to March 2009.

---

## 40.2 Origins

The proximate cause was the collapse of U.S. subprime mortgage markets and structured credit instruments (CDOs, MBS) built on them. The buildup: household debt/GDP rose from 66% (2000) to 99% (2007); investment bank leverage $\Lambda_t = A_t/E_t$ rose from ~15 to ~35; VaR-based risk management with Gaussian loss models grossly underestimated tail risk.

The originate-to-distribute model reduced lender screening incentives; opacity in securitized products prevented market discipline. Regulatory gaps allowed the shadow banking system — money market funds, repo markets, SIVs — to grow to comparable size to the traditional banking system outside prudential oversight.

---

## 40.3 Propagation: The Financial Accelerator

The collapse of MBS valuations reduced financial institution net worth, raising external finance premia, sharply reducing intermediation, and amplifying contraction in investment and consumption. The interbank market froze in September 2008 (LIBOR–OIS spread spiked to 364 bps from a pre-crisis norm of 10–15 bps).

---

## 40.4 Policy Responses

**Monetary:** Fed funds rate from 5.25% (Sep 2007) to 0–0.25% (Dec 2008). Three QE rounds totaling $3.7T. Dollar swap lines to 14 central banks. Emergency credit facilities (TALF, CPFF, PDCF).

**Fiscal:** ARRA (Feb 2009): $787B. Romer–Bernstein (2009) projected multipliers of 1.57 (spending) and 0.99 (tax cuts). Subsequent DSGE-based estimates (Cogan et al., 2010) implied smaller values, generating debate about the adequacy of the stimulus.

**Financial sector:** TARP ($700B authorized, $426B disbursed); SCAP stress tests (March 2009); Dodd–Frank Act (2010): capital requirements, resolution authority, Volcker Rule, mandatory central clearing.

---

## 40.5 Lessons

The slow recovery supports the hysteresis hypothesis. Theoretical lesson: DSGE models without financial frictions could not identify or predict the crisis — the Lucas critique applies to regulatory models as much as to monetary policy models. Macro-financial linkages, heterogeneous agents, and non-Gaussian shocks are quantitatively important.
